Vacant parcels along the D Line extension.

With Los Angeles struggling to approve the housing it needs, I surveyed under-utilized properties around the new D Line extension from March to April 2026, weeks before its first stations opened on May 8.

The line

The D Line extension, west across the corridor

The survey follows this alignment: three existing stations, three that opened in May 2026, and four more under construction through 2027.

Existing (1996) Opened May 2026 Section 2 (Spring 2027) Section 3 (Fall 2027)

Station status and forecasted opening dates: LA Metro, D Line Subway Extension fact sheet (Nov 2025).

Why it matters

Density is coming to this corridor, on one timeline or another

In March 2026, the Los Angeles City Council voted to delay SB 79, the state law that would otherwise allow six- to nine-story housing near major transit stops, by upzoning 55 lower-density areas on the city’s own, lighter terms: buildings of four to sixteen units, up to four stories. The delay runs until 2030, when SB 79 is still set to take effect.

In the meantime, city incentives such as the Opportunity Corridor Transition Area continue to encourage smaller multifamily housing near stations. So the under-utilized parcels in this survey sit in a corridor where denser, transit-oriented housing is being encouraged now and is set to be required by 2030, which is what makes documenting today’s vacant and underused lots worthwhile.

Policy background: Los Angeles Times, “Four-story buildings allowed in some single-family zones under L.A.’s plan to delay SB 79” (March 24, 2026).

How it was done

Methodology

A transparent record of how properties entered the survey, so it can be understood and built on.

Field

Rode the corridor by bike

I biked the corridor around the D Line extension, stopping to photograph any property showing a clear underuse signal: a vacant lot, a vacancy or for-lease sign, surface parking, or a visibly empty or abandoned building. Each photo carries GPS coordinates.

Records

Cross-checked the data

Photographed properties can be matched against county assessor parcel numbers (APNs) and zoning, so individual sites are verifiable against public records.

Boundary

Defined the study area

The survey follows the D Line extension corridor, concentrating on the area around the new and planned stations where transit-oriented housing policy applies.

Limits

What this is and isn’t

A point-in-time field survey based on visible signals from the street, not a legal, appraisal, or exhaustive parcel record. It captures what looked underused on the survey date, not every vacant parcel in the corridor.
