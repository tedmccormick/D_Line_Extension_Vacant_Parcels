Vacant parcels along the D Line extension.

The D Line subway extension opened in May 2026, Los Angeles’s first new heavy rail in 26 years and a hard-won win over the NIMBY forces that fight density. Even with the City Council voting to delay SB 79 (the law legalizing taller housing near transit) for four more years, the case is simple: L.A. needs homes, and infill along transit corridors is the best place to build them. So I biked this one, mapping the vacant lots and underused buildings where that housing could go. Click any green dot to see one.

D Line extension openings. Phase 1: May 8, 2026  ·  Phase 2: Spring 2027  ·  Phase 3: Fall 2027

Backstory

The line that almost never ran down Wilshire

This subway was meant to run down Wilshire decades ago. The original plan sent it west to Fairfax and then north into the San Fernando Valley, until March 1985, when methane rising from the oil field beneath the Fairfax district exploded at a Ross Dress for Less near 3rd and Fairfax. Congressman Henry Waxman, whose district covered the area and whose constituents opposed the subway, used the blast to win a federal ban on tunneling through a Mid-Wilshire “methane zone.”

The line was rerouted north up Vermont Avenue instead, and the Wilshire branch stalled at Western for a generation. Waxman finally championed lifting his own ban in 2007, once engineers showed the tunneling could be done safely. Construction began in 2014, and in May 2026 trains ran beneath Wilshire west of Western for the first time, the corridor mapped above.

Background drawn from Ethan Elkind’s book Railtown: The Fight for the Los Angeles Metro Rail and the Future of the City (UC Press, 2014), and PBS SoCal on the 1985 methane blast.

Why it matters

More subway, more housing

New rail does not just move people; it unlocks a stack of laws meant to put homes by the tracks. California’s SB 79 (effective July 2026) legalizes mid-rise apartments near major transit stops, the State Density Bonus Law trades extra size for affordable units, and AB 2097 scrapped parking minimums near transit. Los Angeles layers its own Transit Oriented Communities program on top.

It is not friction-free. In March 2026 the City Council voted to delay SB 79 until 2030, upzoning 55 areas on lighter terms instead. But the delay is temporary, the incentives keep steering housing toward transit, and these underused lots sit right where the next wave of building is pointed.

How I found them: I biked the corridor around the new stations, photographing every clear sign of underuse (a vacant lot, a for-lease or vacancy sign, surface parking, an empty or shuttered building) and tagged each photo with GPS. It is a point-in-time street survey, not a legal or exhaustive parcel record, but every site can be checked against county assessor parcel numbers and zoning.
